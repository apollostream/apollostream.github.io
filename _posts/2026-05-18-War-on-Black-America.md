Sombering times for me and my family, immediate and extended. We, as Black Americans, feel under siege by our own government again, and as in the past, it's threatening the security of our lives directly in physical attacks by white Americans emboldened by the overt sanctioning by federal officials. Consistent with history, such attacks increase in times when no institutional protections nor legal recourse exist. 

It helps to examine such times clearly and within the context of American history. I did this BFIH analysis 10 days ago to help me to maaintain a clear head. It parsed a trove of evidence to determine the extent to which Conservatism ("architecture"), racism ("animus"), some combination of the two, or something else entirely better explained the entirety of the evidence. I'd sought cause for ease of mind. It did not offer comfort.

__Here's the concluding excerpt from *The Atlantic*-style synopsis of this intellectually honest analysis:__

> It is worth stating plainly what this analysis is and is not. It is not an accusation of guilt in a legal sense; that is a different question with a different burden of proof, decided by different institutions. It is not a claim that every Black American experiences administration policy in the same way; the distributional findings are clear that subpopulations diverge, that Black male Trump voters under forty-five experience the moment differently from Black women federal workers, that Haitian immigrants in Springfield, Ohio face a different policy regime than HBCU students, and that Black voters in formerly Section-2-protected jurisdictions face a different threat than Black senior officials at independent federal agencies. It is not a prediction that every action of the next three years will worsen.
>
> It is the conclusion that emerges when the question’s two embedded claims — animus and architectural attack — are properly distinguished and tested against the available evidence. When that is done, the literal reading of the proposition is what the evidence supports. The strict-architecture-without-animus reading does not survive the racially-specific residual: presidential rhetoric, the personnel pattern, the cross-state cascade, the higher-education channeling, the segregated-facilities clause rescission, the closeness of the Wilson 1912 historical fit. The race-neutral reading does not survive comparison with the actual Reagan, Bush, and first-Trump baselines.
>
> A reader who began with the assumption that “war against Black America” was rhetorical excess should know that the framework most charitable to the administration’s stated framing — beginning with only four percent prior weight on the literal reading — cannot sustain the strict-architecture alternative after sixteen months of evidence. A reader who began with the assumption that the proposition was self-evidently true should know that the work of the analysis was the proper four-cell decomposition that distinguishes what the evidence shows from what could have been claimed without evidence. The proposition is supported in the strong sense the framework allows it to be supported, and the support comes from evidence that cuts across opposing source biases.
>
> That is the answer. It is not a comforting answer, and it is not meant to be. But comfort is not what an honest analysis is for.

### The Primary Proposition

**The proposition to analyze:**

> **_"The Trump administration (since January 20, 2025) is waging a war against Black America."_**

**The upshot:** "Yes".
